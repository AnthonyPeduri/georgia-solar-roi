Why Georgia Solar Math Is Different From Other States

🔄

No True Net Metering

Georgia Power uses the RNR Solar Buy Back program, crediting excess solar at ~7.2¢/kWh — far below what you pay for electricity. States like North Carolina credit at full retail (~12¢/kWh), making their payback periods 2–4 years shorter.

📊

Self-Consumption Is King

Because the export rate is low, maximizing self-consumption is critical in Georgia. Running your dishwasher, laundry, and EV charger during peak solar hours (10am–3pm) dramatically improves ROI versus exporting excess to the grid.

🎫

The 5,000-Spot Cap

Georgia Power's Solar Buy Back program is capped at 5,000 participants statewide — in a state of 11 million people. If the cap fills, new solar customers can't sell back to the grid at any rate. Enrolling sooner protects your ROI.

💵

Federal ITC Still Stacks

The 30% federal ITC (2026, Section 25D) applies regardless of your utility's export rate. On a $24,000 system, that's $7,200 off your taxes. This is a dollar-for-dollar credit — not a deduction — and significantly shortens payback.

Georgia Solar FAQ

Does Georgia have net metering?

Not in the traditional sense. Georgia Power offers the RNR Solar Buy Back program, which credits excess solar at approximately 7.2¢/kWh for 2026. This is below the retail rate you pay (8–14¢/kWh depending on your tier). The program is also capped at 5,000 residential customers statewide.

What is the federal solar tax credit in 2026?

The residential federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) is 30% of total installed system cost under Section 25D. It's a dollar-for-dollar reduction in what you owe in federal income taxes — not just a deduction. The average Georgia system at $24,000 generates a $7,200 tax credit.

What's the typical payback period in Georgia?

Due to the below-retail Solar Buy Back rate, Georgia payback periods typically run 9–13 years for a well-positioned system after the ITC. This is longer than states with full net metering, but most Georgia systems still have 12+ years of net-positive operation remaining after payback.

Is Sawnee EMC better than Georgia Power for solar?

Sawnee EMC (serving Forsyth, Dawson, and surrounding counties) has its own interconnection and buyback policies that may differ from Georgia Power. Some EMCs offer slightly more favorable export rates. This calculator uses Georgia Power's published 2026 rates — contact your specific EMC for their current solar buyback terms.

Does adding a battery improve ROI in Georgia?

Yes, but primarily through bill savings and backup power value — not through better export rates. A battery lets you store midday solar for evening use, avoiding pulling from the grid at retail rates. In Georgia, where export credits are low, batteries make more financial sense than in net-metered states.

What's the maximum system size allowed on Georgia Power?

Georgia Power's Solar Buy Back program caps residential systems at 10 kW AC. Systems larger than 10 kW can still be installed but won't qualify for the export credit program — all excess generation would be wasted. This effectively limits most Georgia residential installs to 10 kW or under.
